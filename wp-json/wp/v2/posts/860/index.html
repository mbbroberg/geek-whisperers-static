<\/a>Big company, small company – does it matter when you’re trying to sway people\u00a0with community and influence marketing? Since influence is earned, does it really matter if you work for a giant church of technology\u00a0or a small startup? Will people\u00a0drive miles for the best chai?<\/p>\n

 <\/p>\n

    \n
  • John says that he had a harder time getting people to pay attention when he was at a startup than when he was at VMware. He likened being a startup to being a tiny mouse standing on a cup.<\/li>\n
  • Matt, now at a startup, says that although he has a much bigger relative share of voice inside his company, his overall potential audience is smaller.<\/li>\n
  • Amy refutes: social media has changed the game, or at least the relative difficulty of using a multiple of tools that make you look bigger than you actually are.<\/li>\n
  • At a small company, everybody has 10 jobs – so there is often no one social media or influencer marketing or evangelist role.<\/li>\n
  • At a small company, goals may be more tactical.<\/li>\n
  • Do you need to be selling big expensive products to afford to hire a community manager?<\/li>\n
  • How does the company even know you’re doing the right thing, contacting the right people?<\/li>\n
  • How does a small company create that “X” factor?<\/li>\n
  • Veeam certainly did it.<\/li>\n
  • Bay area startups are starting to hire community managers very early.<\/li>\n
  • Does influence go with the company or with the person? Can you go from a large company to a small company and keep your influence?<\/li>\n
  • Good influence marketing is relatively cheap, so company size may be irrelevant.<\/li>\n
  • John has been reading a lot of Paul Ford. @ftrain.<\/li>\n
  • tilde.club<\/li>\n
  • Would John Troyer always have been John Troyer without VMware?<\/li>\n
  • Effort = Ability.<\/li>\n
  • The Anna Karenina\u00a0Principle<\/a>: Big companies are all alike, but small companies are all unhappy in their own way. There might be a wide range of influence programs in a small company, but in a big company all influence marketing programs are all alike.<\/li>\n
  • Maybe it’s easier to be influential at a small company where you have more of your own voice rather than having to echo the corporate voice.<\/li>\n
  • The consensus seems to be that the big company ammo is balanced out by small company freedom. Your influence is up to you.<\/li>\n
  • John’s\u00a0key-restaurant-church theory: some companies are like churches, some are like restaurants, and some are like key makers. It’s easier to do influence marketing for a church (often a big company) than a key maker (often a small company)<\/li>\n
  • John’s chai guy, Raman Bechar, differentiates himself by making great chai, and only making it his way.\u00a0Best cup of chai this side of Bombay.<\/a> (He’s moved down the road a bit<\/a> since that article, but is still in Half Moon Bay.)<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n
